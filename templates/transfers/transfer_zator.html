Auschwitz was a complex of Nazi concentration and extermination camps established by Germany during World War II near the town of Oświęcim in Poland. It consisted of three main camps:

  • Auschwitz I: the original camp, opened in 1940, served as the administrative center and housed political prisoners.
  • Auschwitz II (Birkenau): opened in 1941, became the primary site for the mass murder of Jews and other groups; it housed gas chambers and crematoria.
  • Auschwitz III (Monowitz): established in 1942 as a labor camp to supply workers to factories, including the IG Farben chemical plant.

Between 1940 and 1945, approximately 1.3 million people were deported to Auschwitz, of whom about 1.1 million perished. The majority of victims were Jews, along with Poles, Roma, Soviet prisoners of war, and others. The camp was liberated on January 27, 1945, by the Soviet Red Army.
